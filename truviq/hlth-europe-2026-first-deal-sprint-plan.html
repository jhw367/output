HLTH Europe 2026 → First Successful Deal Sprint Plan

Updated: 2026-05-26T13:04:33Z

Requirement

Objective: manage the booth together with Truviq at the HLTH Europe 2026 Conference, network with other exhibitors and sponsors, and generate leads.

Epic: First successful health-sector deal for Truviq originating from the HLTH Europe 2026 campaign.

Working hypothesis: the first deal is most likely not sold directly from the booth. It is created by converting the booth into a campaign engine: pre-event targeting, structured booth conversations, exhibitor/sponsor networking, disciplined qualification, post-event follow-up, discovery, proposal and close.

Definition of “first successful deal”

A deal counts as successful when all of the following are true:

  • A health-sector organisation or implementation partner signs a paid engagement with Truviq.
  • The engagement has a clear workflow/process-orchestration scope, not only generic advisory conversation.
  • The deal can be traced to the HLTH Europe campaign through an event meeting, exhibitor/sponsor introduction, booth lead, or post-event referral.
  • There is a named executive/business sponsor and a named technical/process owner.
  • The first paid step is deliberately sized to reduce buying friction: discovery sprint, proof-of-value, workflow assessment, implementation pilot, or partner-enabled project.

Target first paid offer:

  • Preferred: 4–6 week paid workflow discovery / proof-of-value.
  • Fallback: fixed-fee process-orchestration assessment for a named transformation programme.
  • Avoid: unpaid broad “innovation exploration” with no next-step commitment.

End-to-end scenario: from today to first deal

Phase 0 — Strategic decision and campaign owner

Timing: today → 1 week

Goal: decide whether HLTH Europe is treated as a serious commercial campaign or just event presence.

Key decisions:

  • Who owns the campaign end-to-end: Truviq commercial lead, partner lead, or founder/management?
  • What is the first-deal thesis?

- AZWA/model-plan implementation governance.

- AI workflow governance: speech reporting, capacity planning, digital triage.

- EHDS/identity/consent process orchestration.

- Cyber/process-continuity workflows around EHR dependency.

  • Is Sales-People used as a caller/appointment-setting partner?
  • What is the minimum acceptable event ROI?

- Example: 25 qualified meetings, 8 post-event discoveries, 3 proposals, 1 paid deal.

Exit criteria:

  • One campaign owner named.
  • One primary message selected.
  • One secondary message selected.
  • Decision made on using Sales-People.

Phase 1 — Market thesis and ICP lock

Timing: week 1 → week 2

Goal: define exactly who should be pursued and why they might buy.

ICP segments:

  1. Direct buyers

- Hospitals, UMCs, VVT organisations, insurers, regional care networks.

- Trigger: named transformation programme, digital-care scale-up, AI governance, consent/identity migration, continuity risk.

  1. Ecosystem influencers

- Nictiz, VZVZ, TFHC, Health~Holland, RVO, regional development agencies, sector coalitions.

- Trigger: they know programme owners and implementation routes.

  1. Implementation partners

- Health-IT suppliers, EHR/ECD integrators, data-exchange vendors, consultancy partners, Camunda ecosystem partners.

- Trigger: they need execution capacity around workflow orchestration.

  1. Regulatory/tender monitors

- Organisations or people who surface upcoming procurement windows, standards deadlines, consultations, model-plan owners and grant routes.

Key decisions:

  • Which 50 accounts are worth active pursuit before HLTH?
  • Which 15 are “must meet” at or around HLTH?
  • Which account type receives Sales-People calls?
  • Which accounts should Truviq leadership approach personally instead of outsourcing?

Exit criteria:

  • 50-account target list.
  • Each account has account type, source evidence, hypothesis, likely persona and desired next action.
  • Top 15 accounts marked as “must meet”.

Phase 2 — Offer design and message-market fit

Timing: week 2 → week 3

Goal: make the booth conversation convert into a next meeting, not a vague interest note.

Core offer options:

  1. AI Workflow Governance Sprint

- For hospitals, insurers and providers scaling AI use cases.

- Hook: prevent shadow AI; make AI adoption auditable and operational.

  1. AZWA Model-Plan Execution Sprint

- For insurers, providers and programme teams.

- Hook: turn model-plan funding into controlled intake, monitoring, reporting and accountability workflows.

  1. Consent / Identity / EHDS Workflow Sprint

- For national/regional data exchange programmes and vendors.

- Hook: operationalise consent, access, exceptions, role validation and audit trails.

  1. Care Continuity Workflow Sprint

- For CIO/CISO/RvB/RvT stakeholders.

- Hook: EHR dependency and cyber incidents require rehearsed fallback and recovery workflows.

Decision: choose one primary booth message.

Recommended primary message:

“Truviq helps health organisations make transformation executable: governed workflows for AI, digital care, consent, identity, EHDS and care continuity — without replacing existing EHR/ECD systems.”

Booth CTA:

“Let’s book a 30-minute workflow-fit session after HLTH. We will map one transformation workflow, identify the system boundaries, and show where orchestration creates measurable control.”

Exit criteria:

  • One-page booth pitch.
  • 4 call scripts by account type.
  • 3 email/LinkedIn sequences.
  • Qualification checklist.
  • Meeting booking link and owner.

Phase 3 — Partner operating model with Sales-People

Timing: week 3 → week 4

Goal: make Sales-People useful without letting them overclaim technical capability.

Role of Sales-People:

  • Pre-event calling.
  • Appointment setting.
  • Lead follow-up.
  • Relationship reactivation.
  • Call-note capture.
  • Qualification against source-based hypotheses.

Not their role:

  • Explaining Camunda architecture in depth.
  • Claiming confirmed funding or endorsement.
  • Making healthcare policy interpretations beyond approved script.
  • Handling patient-data or clinical-content discussions.

Key decisions:

  • Go/no-go on Sales-People after a short calibration test.
  • Which account list do they call first?
  • What level of lead qualification is required before a meeting goes into Truviq calendar?
  • What data fields must be captured after every call?

Required call-note fields:

  • Account.
  • Contact name and role.
  • Account type.
  • Trigger referenced.
  • Programme name.
  • Current stack / incumbent systems.
  • Pain confirmed or denied.
  • Procurement route.
  • Event attendance.
  • Meeting accepted / declined / nurture.
  • Next action.
  • Disqualifier if any.

Exit criteria:

  • Sales-People pilot complete on 10 accounts.
  • Call quality reviewed by Truviq.
  • Scripts adjusted.
  • Decision made: scale, limit, or stop.

Phase 4 — Pre-event pipeline build

Timing: 6–8 weeks before HLTH → event week

Goal: arrive at HLTH with meetings already booked.

Target numbers:

  • 100 researched contacts.
  • 50 actively contacted accounts.
  • 25 booked event or post-event meetings.
  • 15 high-priority meetings.
  • 5 partner/referral conversations.

Pre-event campaign motions:

  1. Warm introductions through ecosystem partners.
  2. Sales-People phone qualification for selected Dutch accounts.
  3. Truviq leadership outreach to strategic accounts.
  4. LinkedIn/email sequence tied to HLTH attendance.
  5. Exhibitor/sponsor map: who can refer Truviq into buyers?

Key decisions:

  • Which meetings should happen at booth, coffee area, side event, dinner, or post-event video call?
  • Which accounts deserve custom workflow hypotheses before the meeting?
  • Which contacts should receive a short diagnostic before HLTH?

Exit criteria:

  • Calendar filled with qualified meetings.
  • Booth staffing rota mapped to priority meeting slots.
  • Each priority meeting has a one-page briefing.

Phase 5 — Booth execution at HLTH Europe 2026

Timing: 15–18 June 2026

Goal: make the booth a lead-conversion and intelligence-capture machine.

Booth roles:

  • Host / traffic opener: starts conversations and identifies account type.
  • Qualifier: asks discovery questions and scores fit.
  • Expert: handles workflow/Camunda/process-depth conversations.
  • Scheduler: books next meetings immediately.
  • Recorder: captures notes within 10 minutes of each conversation.

Booth conversation flow:

  1. Identify visitor type: provider, payer, vendor, government/ecosystem, investor, other exhibitor.
  2. Ask: “Which transformation workflow are you trying to make operational this year?”
  3. Select relevant hook: AI, digital triage, AZWA/model plans, consent/identity, EHDS, cyber continuity.
  4. Confirm if the pain is cross-system, regulated, measurable and owned.
  5. If fit: book 30-minute post-event workflow-fit session.
  6. If not fit: tag as partner, referral, nurture or disqualified.

Qualification scoring:

  • +2 named programme.
  • +2 named budget/procurement route.
  • +2 cross-system workflow.
  • +2 compliance/auditability requirement.
  • +1 executive sponsor.
  • +1 technical/process owner.
  • -3 no owner.
  • -3 no workflow pain.
  • -2 only curiosity / innovation tourism.

Lead categories:

  • A: deal candidate — meeting booked within 14 days.
  • B: nurture — real fit, timing unclear.
  • C: partner/referral — can create route to buyers.
  • D: intelligence only.
  • X: disqualified.

Exit criteria:

  • Every qualified booth conversation captured.
  • A-leads have meeting booked before leaving booth or within same day.
  • Daily debrief completed.
  • Top 10 opportunities ranked by end of event.

Phase 6 — Post-event follow-up and discovery conversion

Timing: event week + 1 → + 3 weeks

Goal: convert event interest into serious discovery.

Follow-up SLA:

  • A-leads: same day or next business day.
  • B-leads: within 3 business days.
  • Partner/referral leads: within 5 business days.

Discovery meeting agenda:

  1. Confirm transformation objective.
  2. Map current workflow and systems.
  3. Identify exceptions, handoffs, controls and bottlenecks.
  4. Confirm owner and decision process.
  5. Quantify impact: time, risk, throughput, compliance, cost, waiting list, staffing pressure.
  6. Agree next step: paid sprint, proposal, partner intro, or no fit.

Key decisions:

  • Which accounts move to paid sprint proposal?
  • Which require partner route first?
  • Which are disqualified?
  • What is the first wedge workflow?

Exit criteria:

  • At least 8 discovery meetings completed.
  • At least 3 qualified opportunities accepted into proposal stage.
  • Each proposal has sponsor, problem, scope, value metric and close plan.

Phase 7 — Proposal and close

Timing: event + 3 weeks → event + 12 weeks

Goal: close first paid engagement.

Recommended first-deal structure:

Paid Workflow Discovery Sprint

  • Duration: 4–6 weeks.
  • Scope: one named workflow.
  • Deliverables:

- as-is process map;

- target-state orchestration design;

- integration/system boundary map;

- risk/control/auditability requirements;

- implementation roadmap;

- business case and next-phase proposal.

  • Success metric: agreed next-phase implementation decision or partner-led delivery route.

Key decisions:

  • Fixed-fee vs time-and-materials.
  • Direct buyer vs partner-led sale.
  • Camunda-first implementation vs architecture/discovery first.
  • Whether procurement requires formal tender, light purchase order, framework partner or innovation budget.

Close plan:

  1. Confirm compelling event.
  2. Confirm decision-maker.
  3. Confirm procurement route.
  4. Confirm legal/security blockers.
  5. Send short proposal.
  6. Hold decision meeting.
  7. Resolve objections.
  8. Receive signed order / PO.
  9. Kick off delivery.

Exit criteria:

  • First paid health-sector deal signed.
  • Source tagged as HLTH Europe 2026 campaign.
  • Lessons fed back into repeatable playbook.

Sprint 1 — Campaign foundation

Goal: turn the requirement into an operating plan.

Stories:

  • As Truviq, we need a campaign owner so the event is managed as a revenue motion.
  • As Truviq, we need a clear first-deal thesis so outreach is not generic.
  • As Truviq, we need success metrics so we can decide whether the campaign is working.

Tasks:

  • Name campaign owner and backup.
  • Select primary and secondary message.
  • Define first paid offer.
  • Define KPI ladder.
  • Create campaign workspace and CRM fields.

Sprint decision: proceed only if ownership, message and first-offer are clear.

Done when: campaign brief approved.

Sprint 2 — ICP, account list and evidence

Goal: identify who to meet and why.

Stories:

  • As a caller, I need account hypotheses so I can open with relevance.
  • As a booth team member, I need account type so I know whether I am selling, partnering or gathering intelligence.
  • As Truviq, we need disqualifiers so we do not waste post-event time.

Tasks:

  • Build 50-account target list.
  • Split account types.
  • Add evidence URL per account.
  • Add likely persona per account.
  • Add desired next action.
  • Mark top 15 must-meet accounts.

Sprint decision: approve target list or narrow to a tighter ICP.

Done when: target list is usable for outreach and booth briefings.

Sprint 3 — Offer, scripts and content

Goal: make outreach and booth conversations consistent.

Stories:

  • As Sales-People, we need safe scripts so calls are factual and compliant.
  • As Truviq experts, we need a short explanation of the offer so visitors understand the next step.
  • As prospects, we need a low-friction reason to meet after HLTH.

Tasks:

  • Write 4 call tracks by account type.
  • Create one-page booth pitch.
  • Create 3 outreach email/LinkedIn templates.
  • Create qualification checklist.
  • Create post-event discovery agenda.
  • Create GDPR/claims guardrails.

Sprint decision: approve scripts for caller use.

Done when: Sales-People and Truviq can use the same message without improvising.

Sprint 4 — Sales-People pilot

Goal: prove whether Sales-People can create useful meetings.

Stories:

  • As Truviq, we need to validate call quality before scaling.
  • As Sales-People, we need feedback on which hooks work.
  • As the campaign owner, I need structured call notes.

Tasks:

  • Select 10 pilot accounts.
  • Run caller briefing.
  • Execute calls.
  • Review recordings/notes if available.
  • Score lead quality.
  • Adjust scripts.
  • Decide scale/limit/stop.

Sprint decision: scale Sales-People only if meetings or useful intelligence are produced.

Done when: pilot decision recorded.

Sprint 5 — Pre-event meeting generation

Goal: fill the HLTH calendar before the booth opens.

Stories:

  • As Truviq, we want meetings booked before HLTH so the booth is not dependent on foot traffic.
  • As prospects, we need a specific reason to meet at HLTH.
  • As partners, we need to know what referral would be useful.

Tasks:

  • Launch outreach to 50 accounts.
  • Ask ecosystem partners for introductions.
  • Call priority accounts.
  • Track replies and objections.
  • Book meetings.
  • Prepare one-page brief for each priority meeting.

Sprint decision: if meeting count is low, narrow message or shift from buyers to partners/referrers.

Done when: 25 qualified meetings are booked or a revised target is approved.

Sprint 6 — Booth readiness

Goal: make booth operations measurable.

Stories:

  • As booth staff, we need roles so conversations do not get lost.
  • As the campaign owner, I need every lead captured consistently.
  • As prospects, we need immediate scheduling of next steps.

Tasks:

  • Assign booth roles and shifts.
  • Finalise lead capture form.
  • Prepare scoring model.
  • Prepare daily debrief template.
  • Prepare meeting scheduler.
  • Print / load QR codes and collateral.
  • Rehearse booth conversation flow.

Sprint decision: no booth launch without lead-capture workflow and next-step scheduler.

Done when: booth team can run a simulated visitor conversation end-to-end.

Sprint 7 — HLTH execution

Goal: generate and qualify leads during the event.

Stories:

  • As a visitor, I need to understand in 30 seconds why Truviq is relevant.
  • As Truviq, we need to separate buyers, partners, influencers and noise.
  • As sales owner, I need A-leads booked before they go cold.

Tasks:

  • Run daily stand-up before doors open.
  • Capture all meaningful conversations.
  • Score leads A/B/C/D/X.
  • Book A-lead meetings immediately.
  • Network with exhibitors and sponsors.
  • Run daily debrief.
  • Update top 10 opportunity list each day.

Sprint decision: adjust messaging daily based on observed traction.

Done when: final event debrief and ranked lead list are complete.

Sprint 8 — Post-event discovery

Goal: convert qualified leads into real opportunities.

Stories:

  • As a prospect, I need follow-up that reflects our conversation.
  • As Truviq, we need to discover whether the pain is real and funded.
  • As a deal owner, I need a clear next step after discovery.

Tasks:

  • Follow up A-leads within 1 business day.
  • Hold workflow-fit sessions.
  • Map pain, systems, owner, budget and procurement route.
  • Disqualify weak leads.
  • Advance strong leads to proposal.
  • Update CRM and campaign dashboard.

Sprint decision: select up to 3 proposal candidates.

Done when: 8 discovery meetings are completed and 3 proposal candidates are selected.

Sprint 9 — Proposal and negotiation

Goal: create a closable first paid step.

Stories:

  • As a buyer, I need a low-risk first engagement.
  • As Truviq, we need a paid step that proves value and opens implementation.
  • As procurement, I need scope, price, deliverables and supplier route.

Tasks:

  • Draft proposal for each selected opportunity.
  • Confirm sponsor and decision process.
  • Confirm procurement route.
  • Hold proposal review meeting.
  • Resolve objections.
  • Agree start date and commercial terms.

Sprint decision: choose the deal most likely to close first; concentrate executive attention there.

Done when: first proposal is accepted verbally or procurement process starts.

Sprint 10 — First deal close and handoff

Goal: close and start delivery without losing momentum.

Stories:

  • As the buyer, I need confidence that Truviq can deliver.
  • As Truviq delivery, we need clean handoff from sales.
  • As management, we need lessons for the next deal.

Tasks:

  • Finalise order / contract / PO.
  • Schedule kickoff.
  • Create delivery brief.
  • Transfer discovery notes.
  • Confirm success metrics.
  • Record campaign source.
  • Run retro on HLTH-to-deal motion.

Sprint decision: decide whether to repeat, scale or modify the HLTH campaign pattern.

Done when: first paid engagement has signed commercial commitment and delivery kickoff is scheduled.

Risk register

Risk 1 — Booth traffic but no deal quality

Mitigation: pre-book meetings; do not rely on walk-ups.

Risk 2 — Sales-People creates low-quality meetings

Mitigation: 10-account pilot, strict qualification and call-note review.

Risk 3 — Health buyers see Truviq as too technical

Mitigation: lead with transformation workflow outcomes, not Camunda internals.

Risk 4 — Event audience is not the actual procurement owner

Mitigation: use exhibitors/sponsors/ecosystem contacts to map decision chains.

Risk 5 — AZWA model plans reduce bespoke budget

Mitigation: position Truviq as execution/governance layer around standardised programmes.

Risk 6 — Compliance/claims issue in outreach

Mitigation: approved scripts; factual claims only; no patient data; opt-out logging.
